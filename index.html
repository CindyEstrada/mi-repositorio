The Gunadule´s Sacred Geometry

Let's learn something about the worldview of the indigenous people called Gunadules. They have preserved much of their ancestral culture despite centuries of colonization and interaction with other cultures around the world.

The respect they have for women is remarkable, there are rituals of the coming-of-age of girls and one of the main elements to protect them is the Mola. There are many protection Molas, these are visual representations of sacred narratives associated with the stars, animals, plants or minerals.

According to Wikipedia (2009) Sacred Geometry "ascribes symbolic and sacred meanings to certain geometric shapes and certain geometric proportions. It is associated with the belief that a god is the geometer of the world. The geometry used in the design and construction of religious structures such as churches, temples, mosques, religious monuments, altars, and tabernacles has sometimes been considered sacred. The concept applies also to sacred spaces such as temenoi, sacred groves, village greens, pagodas and holy wells, and the creation of religious art."

So, interpreting it from my point of view, the Molas are sacred geometry that protects women and therefore life on earth.

Where Gunadules live?

The self-denomination: Gunadule, is interpreted in their language as 'Men and women from the surface of the earth' (Castaño, Santacruz, 2012). So, we could say that we are all Gunadule. Although the community itself, the Guna people "live in three politically autonomous comarcas or autonomous reservations in Panama, and in a few small villages in Colombia. There are also communities of Guna people in Panama City, Colón, and other cities. Most Gunas live on small islands off the coast of the comarca of Guna Yala known as the San Blas Islands. The other two Guna comarcas in Panama are Kuna de Madugandíand Kuna de Wargandí. They are Guna-speaking people who once occupied the central region of what is now Panama and the neighboring San Blas Islands and still survive in marginal areas" (Wikipedia, 2020).

Although the political separation between Colombia and Panama has affected them as a community since 1903, "the strength of their traditions and language maintain them together" (Gutiérrez, 2018).

How do Molas protect women?

Goaniggadi Mola

Gunadule women learn to weave Molas from an early age together with their mothers. "The Goaniggadi Mola, corresponds to the figurative Molas with common elements of nature and are used in everyday life" in women's clothing: shirts, shoes, bags... the images emerge from the creativity of each weaver. These molas are very commercial and have given economic support and global recognition to the community.

Woman weaving a figurative Mola. Click on the image to know more.

Mola Naga

The "Mola Naga corresponds to the Molas of Protection, Molas associated with the origin stories of the Gunadule people, which have their own name: Yala Burba Mola, Nibali Mola, Naggurus Mola, among others; and they are used mainly in the ceremonies of the passage from girl to woman. They are used in the puberty parties Sumba Inna: the first menstruation and Inna Dummadi: ritual of freedom, also in Iggo Inna: ritual of the nose ring (Castaño, Santacruz, 2012).
